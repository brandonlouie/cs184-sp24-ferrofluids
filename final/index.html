Abstract

Ferrofluids are fluids containing magnetic particles that react to the magnetic field surrounding them, generally introduced by magnets or induced via changes in electrical currents. Ferrofluids were first invented by NASA in 1963 as a way to transport rocket fuel in weightless environments, and the current applications of these fluids in the real world involve biosensing, medical imaging, energy harvesting and transfer, and vibration control. In this project, we attempt to simulate the behavior of ferrofluids, which would allow us to study its unique properties and apply it to many of the aforementioned real world applications. On this webpage, you will see our attempts at simulating basic ferrofluid-magnet interactions, where the fluid forms characteristic spikes reacting to introduced magnetic fields. There were three major parts to this project: fluid simulation (creating a basic fluid, represented as a collection of particles), magnetization (introducing magnets into our simulation and magnetizing/"ferro-izing" our fluid), and cube marching (creating a mesh over these particles to make our simulation better resemble real-world fluids).


Technical Approach

Our project uses Homework 4: Cloth Sim from UC Berkeley's Computer Graphics and Imaging course (CS184) as a basis for our fluid simulation. This homework assignment provides the framework for mathematically based physical simulations, and more specifically focused on the simulation of cloth and its interactions with planes, spheres, and itself. We chose to us this assignment as our starting framework as our project is on a particle-based simulation, which is precisely what we implemented in this assignment; just as we can represent a cloth as a collection of particles connected with springs, we can express our fluid as a collection of particles that interact with each other with binding forces similar to springs. The major we made to this existing repository is updating the object to be simulated; we effectively replaced every file or instance specific to cloth simulation with our own files and definitions that specify the existence of a fluid object. The structure for our particle-based representation of fluids is heavily inspired "A Current Loop Model for the Fast Simulation of Ferrofluids" by Shao, Huang, and Michels (linked below).
